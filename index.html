üîë Key Concepts

This week's concepts help us understand how race is lived‚Äînot as an idea or category, but as an embodied, atmospheric, habitual dimension of experience that shapes perception, movement, and possibility.

üëÅÔ∏è

The Racial Gaze

Being seen and fixed by the look of the white Other. Fanon describes being "sealed" into his body by this gaze‚Äîmade into an object rather than a subject.

üîÑ

Racist Habits

Automatic, pre-reflective bodily responses shaped by racist socialization. These operate below conscious awareness‚Äîin how we perceive, move toward or away from, and respond to racialized others.

üå´Ô∏è

Racial Atmosphere

The felt "tone" or "mood" of a space shaped by its racial dynamics. Some spaces feel welcoming, others hostile or alienating‚Äîoften without explicit signage.

üé≠

Double Consciousness

Du Bois's concept describing the experience of seeing oneself through the eyes of a racist society while also knowing one's own truth‚Äîa split awareness.

üö´

Bodily Alienation

The experience of being estranged from one's own body‚Äîwhen the body that is "seen" by others doesn't match one's own lived sense of self.

üìñ

Counter-Story

Narratives that challenge dominant stories about race. A methodological tool for centering marginalized experiences and disrupting majoritarian narratives.

üîÆ Critical Race Methodology & Phenomenology

This week introduces a powerful synergy: combining Critical Race Methodology with phenomenological analysis. Sol√≥rzano and Yosso's framework provides tools for centering race that complement phenomenology's attention to lived experience.

Five Elements of Critical Race Methodology

1. Centrality of Race and Racism

Race and racism are central, permanent, and endemic features of society‚Äînot aberrations. Research must foreground race, not treat it as one variable among many.

For phenomenology: How does race shape the structures of experience itself‚Äîperception, embodiment, temporality, space?

2. Challenge to Dominant Ideology

CRT challenges claims of objectivity, meritocracy, colorblindness, and race neutrality. These ideologies disguise the self-interest of dominant groups.

For phenomenology: What gets called "universal" experience? Whose lifeworld is taken as the norm?

3. Commitment to Social Justice

Research should work toward eliminating racism, sexism, and poverty‚Äîtoward liberation rather than merely understanding.

For phenomenology: How can phenomenological insight serve emancipatory ends? What changes when we truly understand racialized experience?

4. Centrality of Experiential Knowledge

The experiential knowledge of people of color is legitimate, appropriate, and critical for understanding racism. Lived experience is a source of knowledge, not just data.

For phenomenology: This aligns perfectly‚Äîphenomenology privileges first-person experience as the ground of all knowledge.

5. Interdisciplinary Perspective

CRT draws from multiple disciplines‚Äîhistory, sociology, law, ethnic studies‚Äîto analyze race and racism.

For phenomenology: Critical phenomenology similarly draws on philosophy, psychology, critical theory, and lived experience.

Counter-Stories in Phenomenological Research

Counter-stories are a key methodological tool in CRT. They can be used in phenomenological research to:

  • Challenge majoritarian stories about educational "problems" and "achievement gaps"
  • Center the perspectives of those typically studied as objects, not subjects
  • Reveal what standardized data cannot capture‚Äîthe meaning of educational experiences
  • Create space for truths that dominant narratives exclude

üí≠ Connecting to Your Research

  1. What educational phenomenon do you want to understand? Whose lived experience of this phenomenon is typically centered? Whose is marginalized?
  2. What "majoritarian stories" circulate about this phenomenon? What counter-stories might you help surface?
  3. How might critical phenomenology help you study this phenomenon differently than traditional qualitative approaches?
